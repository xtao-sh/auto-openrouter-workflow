## Referee Report — Field Expert

**Date**: 2026-03-26
**Paper**: "Creative Destruction in the Market for Intelligence: Demand Reallocation When New LLMs Enter"

### 总体评价（Summary Assessment）

This paper studies demand reallocation following new model entry on OpenRouter, a major LLM API aggregation platform. Using daily data on 385 models from 66 firms over 93 days (December 2025–March 2026), the authors estimate a Berry (1994) nested logit demand model and conduct descriptive panel regressions and event studies around entry events. The headline finding is that creative destruction operates almost exclusively through within-family upgrades: when a firm releases a successor in the same product family, the predecessor loses roughly 35% of daily requests, while different-family entries and cross-firm entries produce no detectable displacement. The OLS nesting parameter of 0.46 indicates moderate within-firm substitution; alternative nesting by capability tier and price tier yields near-boundary estimates above 0.99.

The paper's main strengths are: (i) an interesting and timely market setting where the speed of Schumpeterian dynamics is remarkable; (ii) intellectual honesty about the descriptive nature of the exercise—the authors are unusually forthcoming about identification limitations, the IV failure, and external validity concerns; (iii) the combination of a structural demand model and descriptive panel regressions, where the concordance between the two provides mutual validation; and (iv) a sensible decomposition framework that yields an economically interpretable "quality ladder" narrative. The writing is clean, the institutional description is accurate, and the robustness checks are thorough.

The main problems are: (i) the structural and reduced-form components are each individually underpowered or underidentified—the nested logit has no valid instrument, and the panel regressions are explicitly not causal; (ii) the paper's central conceptual framing ("creative destruction") overpromises relative to what the evidence can deliver—the absence of cross-firm displacement over 93 days in a rapidly growing market is consistent with many stories besides Schumpeterian dynamics; (iii) the alternative nesting results (sigma > 0.99) are almost certainly mechanical artifacts of specification rather than substantive findings, yet the paper interprets them at face value; and (iv) the paper does not adequately engage with the multihoming and simultaneous usage patterns that are central to the OpenRouter setting (and that Fradkin 2025 emphasizes).

### 逐项评分

1. **文献定位与边际贡献：6/10** — The paper positions itself relative to Fradkin (2025), Demirer et al. (2025), and Bresnahan & Greenstein (1999), which is correct. The claimed marginal contribution over Fradkin—systematic panel analysis of all entries plus a structural demand model—is genuine but oversold. Fradkin's three case studies provide richer institutional detail on specific events; this paper trades depth for breadth, but the breadth is somewhat shallow: the aggregate null is precisely estimated but economically uninteresting, and the "action" is in 43 family-upgrade events, which is not dramatically more than Fradkin's three. The paper does not engage with the broader IO literature on product line cannibalization (e.g., Desai 2001 on auto model proliferation, Draganska & Jain 2005 on yogurt line extensions) or with the quality-ladder endogenous growth literature (Aghion & Howitt 1992, Grossman & Helpman 1991 quality ladder model) at the depth one would expect from a paper titled "Creative Destruction." The connection to Bresnahan & Greenstein is repeatedly invoked but remains at the level of a paragraph-length comparison; a more serious engagement would discuss what features of market structure (switching costs, compatibility, network effects) explain the speed differential.

2. **制度背景准确性：7/10** — The description of OpenRouter, API pricing, and the LLM ecosystem is largely accurate and well-informed. The paper correctly identifies the near-zero switching cost feature as both a strength and a limitation. However, several institutional details are missing or imprecise. First, OpenRouter is not just a routing layer; it also caches responses, applies rate limits, and offers its own pricing markup—these features affect the observed data in ways the paper does not discuss. Second, the paper does not discuss the role of "free" or "subsidized" model tiers that some providers offer through OpenRouter, which affects the interpretation of request counts as demand signals. Third, the family classification based on string matching of model identifiers is described as "necessarily imperfect" but the paper does not discuss specific cases where this fails (e.g., OpenAI's naming shifts from GPT-4 to GPT-4o to o1 to o3, which break simple string-matching logic). Fourth, the claim that OpenRouter serves "over one million developers" is taken from the platform's marketing materials without discussion of what fraction are active.

3. **研究问题重要性：8/10** — The question "where does new model demand come from?" is genuinely important for understanding innovation incentives, market structure evolution, and developer risk management in the LLM market. The three-channel decomposition (cannibalization / displacement / expansion) is a natural and useful framework. The question matters both for positive economics (understanding market dynamics) and for practical decisions by firms and developers. The main reservation is scope: the paper frames this as a study of "creative destruction," which is a grand concept, but what the data actually deliver is a much more specific finding about within-family upgrades on a single platform over three months. The mismatch between the framing and the deliverable is a persistent tension.

4. **结果经济学解读：5/10** — This is the weakest dimension. Several interpretive issues need to be addressed. First, the null on cross-firm displacement is attributed to "horizontal differentiation" and the market being in a "growth phase," but the paper does not seriously adjudicate between these explanations. If the market is growing 20% in 93 days, the null on cross-firm displacement may simply reflect that everyone's demand is rising and there is no zero-sum reallocation to detect—this is a market-expansion story, not a differentiation story. The paper conflates the two. Second, the alternative nesting results (sigma > 0.99 for capability and price tiers) are interpreted as "models within the same capability or price segment compete more intensely," but this is almost certainly a mechanical result. With only 2 capability nests or 4 price nests, the within-group share has enormous cross-sectional variation driven by heterogeneity within groups; the authors themselves acknowledge this possibility but then proceed to interpret the estimates at face value. Third, the "concordance" between the nested logit (sigma = 0.46) and the panel regression (family-upgrade coefficient of -0.43) is presented as mutual validation, but these are measuring fundamentally different things: sigma measures preference correlation across all same-firm models continuously, while the panel coefficient measures the discrete effect of a specific type of entry event on predecessor usage. The fact that they are "in the same ballpark" numerically is coincidental. Fourth, the Oster delta of 155 is presented as strong evidence of robustness, but the Oster test addresses a different form of bias (omitted variable bias from unobservables correlated with observables) than the specific endogeneity concern here (simultaneity in shares). The paper should not lean on this as heavily as it does.

5. **前沿文献对话：5/10** — The paper cites the right core papers but does not engage deeply with them. The engagement with Fradkin (2025) is mostly "we do what he does, but for all events"—this misses Fradkin's key insight about multihoming patterns, which is directly relevant to interpreting substitution on OpenRouter. If developers multihome across models (which Fradkin documents extensively), then "requests" at the model level do not correspond to the discrete-choice framework the nested logit assumes—a single developer can send requests to multiple models simultaneously. This is a fundamental tension the paper does not address. The engagement with Demirer et al. (2025) is similarly thin: they document price elasticities near unity, but the paper's own elasticity estimates are somewhat lower and the reconciliation is hand-waved. The paper does not cite or engage with the growing literature on AI model evaluation and benchmarking (e.g., Zheng et al. 2024 on Chatbot Arena), which is directly relevant to understanding demand formation. The paper also does not engage with the platform economics literature on aggregators and intermediaries (e.g., Hagiu & Wright 2015 on multi-sided platforms) despite OpenRouter being precisely such an intermediary.

### 总分：6.2/10

### Major Comments（必须回应）

1. **The discrete-choice framework is inconsistent with multihoming behavior.** The nested logit assumes each "consumer" (developer) chooses one model per choice occasion. But on OpenRouter, developers routinely send requests to multiple models—for A/B testing, fallback routing, or task-specific routing. Fradkin (2025) documents this explicitly. If a single developer sends 100 requests to Claude and 50 to GPT-4o on the same day, the nested logit treats this as 150 separate "consumers" each making a discrete choice, which misattributes within-developer portfolio allocation as between-developer preference heterogeneity. This matters because the nesting parameter sigma will reflect both genuine preference similarity (developers who like Claude also like other Anthropic models) and mechanical portfolio correlation (developers who use Anthropic at all tend to use multiple Anthropic models because they are testing within-family). The paper should: (a) explicitly discuss this limitation; (b) provide evidence on the degree of multi-model usage in the data (e.g., what fraction of total requests come from models that are the sole model used in their firm on a given day?); and (c) discuss whether a model of demand with multihoming (e.g., à la Gentzkow 2007 on newspaper bundling, or a multiple-discrete-choice model) would be more appropriate.

2. **The "creative destruction" framing is too strong for the evidence.** Schumpeterian creative destruction implies that new entrants destroy incumbent rents, reallocating economic surplus. What the paper actually documents is within-family cannibalization in a growing market—closer to planned obsolescence or product line management than to creative destruction. The predecessor model being replaced by its successor within the same firm is not "destruction" in the Schumpeterian sense; it is a firm upgrading its own product. True creative destruction would involve cross-firm displacement, which the paper finds no evidence of. The paper should either: (a) reframe around "product line cannibalization" and "quality ladder dynamics," which is what the evidence actually shows; or (b) seriously engage with why the absence of cross-firm displacement is or is not inconsistent with the creative destruction narrative, drawing on the distinction between "creative destruction" (Schumpeter Mark I, entrepreneurial) and "creative accumulation" (Schumpeter Mark II, incumbent-driven innovation).

3. **The IV failure deserves more treatment and the OLS sigma needs better bounding.** The BLP instrument fails spectacularly (negative sigma), and the paper correctly diagnoses the reason (product-line breadth correlates with firm quality). But the paper then simply defaults to the OLS estimate and leans on the Oster bound. This is insufficient. The Oster bound addresses omitted variable bias from additional controls, not the simultaneity in share equations that is the primary concern. The paper should: (a) discuss the sign and magnitude of the expected OLS bias more formally—the mechanical upward bias from positive demand shocks raising both s_mt and s_{m|g,t} is well understood in the BLP literature, and even a rough calibration would be informative; (b) consider alternative instruments: Hausman-type instruments (average within-firm share of the same model in other time periods), or characteristics of other firms' models as instruments for within-firm share; (c) consider estimating the model by GMM using BLP-style moment conditions with multiple instruments, even if individually weak; (d) at minimum, provide an honest range for sigma that accounts for the likely upward OLS bias, rather than presenting 0.46 as a point estimate with a precise standard error.

4. **The alternative nesting results (sigma > 0.99) are likely mechanical and should not be interpreted substantively.** With only 2 nests (reasoning vs. non-reasoning) or 4 nests (price quartiles), the within-group share s_{m|g,t} varies enormously across models because each nest contains extremely heterogeneous products. In this situation, the cross-sectional correlation between ln(s_mt) - ln(s_0t) and ln(s_{m|g,t}) is driven primarily by size differences across models within the same broad group, not by genuine within-group substitution. The boundary estimate sigma = 0.999 almost certainly reflects this mechanical correlation. The paper acknowledges this possibility in one sentence but then proceeds to draw substantive conclusions from these estimates ("the deeper competitive margin is defined by what models do and what they cost"). The paper should: (a) show that the result survives with more granular nests (e.g., 8-10 capability-price cells); (b) report a specification test comparing the fit of the 2-nest model to the 66-nest (firm) model; (c) estimate a multi-level nested logit (capability tier > firm) to separate the two margins; or (d) drop or substantially downgrade these results, presenting them as illustrative rather than as a key finding.

5. **The event study for family upgrades is underpowered and the dynamic pattern is not clearly established.** The paper reports that only 8 of 43 family-upgrade events have sufficient pre- and post-event data within the 93-day panel. This is a very small number of events for an event study, and the paper's verbal description of the dynamic pattern ("coexistence-then-displacement") is doing more work than the data support. With 8 events and wide confidence intervals, the specific timing of displacement (immediate vs. delayed) is not well-identified. The paper should: (a) show individual event-level plots for the major upgrade events (Claude 3.5 to 3.7, GPT-4o iterations, Gemini upgrades) so the reader can assess heterogeneity; (b) consider relaxing the event window requirements to include more events, accepting shorter pre-periods; (c) be more conservative in the verbal interpretation—"we cannot distinguish immediate from gradual displacement" is more honest than "coexistence-then-displacement."

6. **The outside option construction needs more scrutiny.** The paper sets s_0t = 0.20 and shows that sigma is invariant to this choice (10%, 20%, 40%). The invariance of sigma is expected because sigma is identified from within-day cross-sectional variation. But other parameters—particularly the price coefficient alpha and the implied own-price elasticities—do depend on the outside option share. The paper should: (a) report how the price coefficient and implied elasticities vary with the outside option assumption; (b) discuss what the "outside option" actually represents in this context (non-OpenRouter API usage? not using LLMs at all?); (c) consider whether the outside option share should vary over time as the market grows, rather than being fixed.

### Minor Comments（建议回应）

1. The paper reports N = 29,084 in Table 2 (descriptive statistics) but N = 30,903 for the panel regressions and N = 26,444 for the nested logit. The text explains the different samples, but a clear table showing which filters produce which sample sizes would improve transparency.

2. The variable SameFamilyUpgrade in Table 1 is defined as an indicator, but the same-family upgrade coefficient in Table 4 appears to be estimated from a specification where it enters as a count. The paper should clarify: is it an indicator (0/1) for "any same-family upgrade occurred in the past 7 days" or a count of such events?

3. The within-R-squared values of 0.000–0.006 in Table 3 are remarkably low. The paper correctly notes this is expected, but it also raises the question of what drives within-model usage variation. A brief decomposition of variance (how much is model FE, how much is day FE, how much is residual) would be informative.

4. The paper uses ln(requests + 1) as the dependent variable. For models with very low usage (near zero), the +1 transformation can distort magnitudes. A Poisson pseudo-maximum-likelihood specification (Santos Silva & Tenreyro 2006) would be more appropriate for count data with many near-zero observations.

5. The price variable appears to be time-invariant (set at model listing). If providers adjust prices over the sample period—which some do—the nested logit is using stale prices. The paper should discuss whether and how prices changed during the sample.

6. The "model age" variable in the nested logit is measured as days since first appearance in the panel, not days since actual model release. For models that were active before the panel begins in December 2025, this variable is left-censored. This affects both the point estimate on age and the residual.

7. The first-stage F-statistic of 4,961 for the IV specification is enormous, suggesting the instrument is strong in the statistical sense but violates the exclusion restriction. This is a textbook case of a "strong but invalid instrument," and the paper should frame it this way explicitly rather than just noting the negative sigma.

8. The paper mentions a pre-analysis plan but does not provide a link or registration number. For credibility, the PAP should be publicly posted or at least referenced with sufficient detail for verification.

### 遗漏文献

- **Gentzkow (2007), "Valuing New Goods in a Model with Complementarity: Online Newspapers"** — Directly relevant for modeling demand with multihoming/multi-product usage, which is the central limitation of the discrete-choice framework applied here.

- **Desai (2001), "Quality Segmentation in Spatial Markets: When Does Cannibalization Matter?"** and **Draganska & Jain (2005), "Product-Line Length as a Competitive Tool"** — The within-family cannibalization finding maps directly onto the product line management literature, which the paper does not cite.

- **Aghion & Howitt (1992), "A Model of Growth through Creative Destruction"** — The paper cites Aghion & Howitt (2005) but not the foundational 1992 QJE paper that defines the quality-ladder framework the paper's narrative relies on.

- **Zheng et al. (2024), "Judging LLM-as-a-Judge with MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena"** — Understanding how developers evaluate model quality (through benchmarks, arena rankings) is relevant to understanding demand formation and switching behavior.

- **Hagiu & Wright (2015), "Multi-Sided Platforms"** — OpenRouter is an intermediary/aggregator platform, and the paper should engage with the theory of how platform structure affects observed competition patterns.

- **Santos Silva & Tenreyro (2006), "The Log of Gravity"** — Relevant for the econometric treatment of count data with the ln(x+1) transformation.

- **Sun & Abraham (2021), "Estimating Dynamic Treatment Effects in Event Studies with Heterogeneous Treatment Effects"** — The identification memo mentions this as a potential robustness check, but the paper does not implement it. Given the staggered treatment concern, this is an important omission.

- **Nevo (2001), "Measuring Market Power in the Ready-to-Eat Cereal Industry"** — The standard reference for dealing with the BLP instrument validity concern that the paper encounters.
