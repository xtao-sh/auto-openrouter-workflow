## Referee Report — Econometrician

**Date**: 2026-03-26
**Paper**: "Creative Destruction in the Market for Intelligence: Demand Reallocation When New LLMs Enter"

### 总体评价（Summary Assessment）

This paper studies demand reallocation when new LLMs enter the OpenRouter API marketplace, using 93 days of daily model-level usage data covering 385 models from 66 firms. The analysis has two prongs: (1) a nested logit demand model (Berry 1994) that estimates a within-firm nesting parameter of 0.46, indicating moderately closer substitution among same-firm models; and (2) descriptive panel regressions showing that the aggregate same-firm entry effect is null, but decomposition reveals a large (-0.43 log points, or ~35%) displacement effect concentrated in within-family upgrades. The paper claims that creative destruction in this market operates almost exclusively along "quality ladders" within model families.

The paper's principal methodological strength is its honesty about what it can and cannot identify. The authors explicitly acknowledge that the panel regressions are descriptive rather than causal, that the nested logit relies on cross-sectional share variation rather than exogenous price or quality variation, and that the IV strategy fails. The pre-analysis plan and identification memo demonstrate commendable transparency. The dual-strategy approach — structural demand estimation for substitution patterns plus reduced-form event studies for dynamic adjustment — is sensible in a setting where clean causal identification is unavailable.

However, several methodological concerns are serious. The IV failure (producing a negative, out-of-range nesting parameter) is more problematic than the paper acknowledges, because it implies that the OLS estimate of sigma is potentially severely biased by simultaneity. The Oster bound, while reassuring for omitted-variable bias from observables, does not address the mechanical simultaneity between market shares and within-firm shares. More critically, the pre-trend test statistics reported in the results summary file (t = -2.46 for same-firm, t = -2.05 for cross-firm, t = -2.75 for family upgrades) indicate statistically significant pre-trends, directly contradicting the paper's repeated claim of "no evidence of differential pre-trends." This is a first-order credibility issue for the event study evidence. Additionally, the near-unity nesting parameters (>0.99) for capability-tier and price-tier groupings are almost certainly artifacts of mechanical correlation rather than substantive findings, and the paper's acknowledgment of this is insufficient.

### 逐项评分

1. **识别策略可信度：5/10** — The paper is admirably honest about lacking causal identification, which itself deserves credit. The nested logit is identified from cross-sectional variation in shares conditional on observables and day fixed effects, which is standard but vulnerable to unobserved quality differences across models. The panel regressions condition on model and day FE, which is the appropriate TWFE structure, but the key identification assumption — that entry timing is uncorrelated with model-specific demand shocks conditional on FE — is untestable and potentially violated (firms may time releases strategically). The IV failure is revealing: the most natural instrument (product-line breadth) violates exclusion. The paper leans heavily on descriptive framing to avoid overclaiming, which is appropriate, but the family-upgrade coefficient of -0.43 is still presented as the "core finding" and discussed as if it has causal content (e.g., "the predecessor is associated with approximately 35% fewer daily requests"). The significant pre-trend t-statistics undermine even the descriptive interpretation.

2. **计量模型设定：6/10** — The nested logit specification follows Berry (1994) faithfully and is appropriate for this setting. The choice of firm-based nesting is well-motivated. Day fixed effects absorb aggregate shocks; the exclusion of model FE from the nested logit is necessary given time-invariant characteristics. The panel regression with model and day FE is standard TWFE. However, the entry variable construction — counting new models in a trailing 7-day window — is coarse and potentially problematic. The same-firm entry count has SD = 8.4, meaning a firm releasing 8+ models in a week is not uncommon, which conflates major flagship launches with minor model variants. The family-upgrade indicator is more precise but relies on string-matching of model names, which the authors acknowledge is imperfect. The log(requests + 1) transformation is standard but introduces well-known bias at the boundary (models with zero or near-zero requests). The paper does not discuss whether the log-linear specification is appropriate given the extreme right-skew (mean/median ratio of 29:1).

3. **标准误与推断：5/10** — Standard errors are clustered at the model level (385 clusters), which is appropriate for serial correlation within model time series. However, several issues arise. First, the pre-trend t-statistics in the results summary (t = -2.46 for same-firm, t = -2.75 for family upgrades) are significant at the 5% level, yet the paper states "no evidence of differential pre-trends." This discrepancy must be explained. Second, the paper does not report firm-level clustered standard errors for the nested logit in the main table, though the summary file shows the firm-clustered SE for sigma is 0.074 (vs. 0.042 with model clustering) — nearly double. Third, the family-upgrade coefficient is based on only 43 events, and the event study uses only 8 events with full windows. With such small effective sample sizes, inference is fragile. Fourth, no multiple testing correction is applied despite testing effects across multiple entry types, heterogeneity dimensions, and robustness checks.

4. **稳健性检验：6/10** — The robustness battery is reasonably comprehensive: alternative DVs (tokens, market share, rolling average), sample restrictions (high-volume, trimmed edges), placebo (shuffled firms), Oster bounds, leave-one-firm-out, and alternative nesting structures. However, several critical robustness checks are missing. (a) There is no robust estimator for staggered TWFE (Sun & Abraham or Callaway & Sant'Anna), despite the identification memo explicitly listing this as a planned mitigation. (b) There is no two-way clustering (model × week), despite the pre-analysis plan specifying this. (c) The placebo test shuffles firm labels but not entry dates — a date-shuffling placebo would directly test whether the family-upgrade result is spurious. (d) The alternative nesting structures produce boundary estimates (sigma > 0.99) that are clearly pathological, yet the paper extracts substantive conclusions from them. (e) No bootstrap or permutation inference is reported for the family-upgrade coefficient.

5. **统计 vs 经济显著性：7/10** — This is one of the paper's stronger areas. The authors explicitly compute the economic magnitude of the family-upgrade effect (35% decline in requests), benchmark it against Fradkin (2025)'s case studies, and provide a structural validation by comparing the reduced-form estimate to the nested logit prediction. The null results are also given economic interpretation with confidence interval bounds. The discussion of within-R-squared (essentially zero) is honest and appropriate. However, the paper could do more to quantify the revenue implications of the family-upgrade effect (using the pricing data available) and to discuss whether 35% displacement in 7 days is "large" relative to other technology markets in comparable units.

6. **内生性讨论：7/10** — The paper's discussion of endogeneity is among its strongest features. Three threats are explicitly identified (correlated demand shocks, staggered treatment, market expansion confounding), each with specific mitigations. The IV failure is discussed candidly rather than buried. The identification memo provides additional depth on timing exogeneity and the distinction between structural and descriptive identification. The paper correctly notes that reverse causality (declining demand triggering a rush release) would bias against finding displacement, not toward it. However, the discussion does not adequately address simultaneity bias in the nested logit OLS estimate of sigma, which is the most fundamental endogeneity concern for the structural estimates.

### 总分：5.8/10

### Major Comments（必须回应）

1. **Pre-trends are significant and the paper misrepresents them.** The results summary file reports pre-trend t-statistics of -2.46 (same-firm), -2.05 (cross-firm), and -2.75 (family upgrades), all significant at the 5% level or close to it. Yet the paper claims "no evidence of differential pre-trends" and states that "pre-trend coefficients are noisy but centered near zero." This is a serious misrepresentation. The significant pre-trends for the family-upgrade event study (t = -2.75) directly undermine the key finding: if predecessor models were already declining before the upgrade event, the post-event coefficient of -0.43 overstates (or entirely spuriously captures) the entry effect. The authors must (a) report the formal pre-trend test statistics in the paper, (b) explain the discrepancy between these statistics and the qualitative claims, and (c) conduct a bias-correction exercise (e.g., Roth 2022, "Pre-test with Caution") to assess how much of the -0.43 coefficient could be explained by pre-existing trends.

2. **The OLS nesting parameter sigma = 0.46 lacks a credible identification strategy.** The simultaneity between s_mt and s_{m|g,t} is a textbook endogeneity problem in nested logit estimation. A positive unobserved demand shock to model m raises both its market share and its within-firm share, biasing sigma upward. The paper's IV (count of same-firm models) produces a negative, out-of-range estimate, confirming that the endogeneity is severe and the instrument is invalid. The Oster bound addresses selection on observables but not this mechanical simultaneity. The paper should (a) attempt alternative instruments — e.g., characteristics of other firms' models (Hausman-type instruments), lagged within-firm shares, or leave-out shares constructed by excluding model m from its firm's total; (b) estimate bounds on sigma under different assumptions about the direction and magnitude of bias; and (c) discuss what the IV result (sigma_IV = -0.15) actually implies about the correlation structure between the instrument and unobservables.

3. **The family-upgrade result relies on only 43 events (8 in the event study), and no permutation inference is conducted.** With 43 upgrade events driving the key finding, standard asymptotic inference (cluster-robust SEs assuming normality) may be unreliable. The event study figure uses only 8 events with full pre/post windows, which is insufficient for credible visual evidence. The authors should (a) report a permutation/randomization inference p-value for the -0.43 coefficient by randomly reassigning upgrade dates and recomputing the coefficient many times; (b) report wild cluster bootstrap p-values (Cameron, Gelbach, and Miller 2008) given the small number of effective clusters contributing to identification; and (c) examine whether the result is driven by one or two large upgrade events by conducting leave-one-event-out sensitivity.

4. **The near-unity nesting parameters for capability and price tiers are mechanical artifacts, not substantive findings.** When models are grouped into 2 nests (reasoning vs. non-reasoning) or 4 nests (price quartiles), the within-group heterogeneity is enormous. The within-group share s_{m|g,t} then varies primarily because of model-level quality differences within groups, not because of meaningful within-group substitution. The regression of (ln s_mt - ln s_0t) on ln s_{m|g,t} in this setting recovers a coefficient near 1.0 because both variables are driven by the same unobserved model quality. The authors should either (a) drop these results entirely, (b) estimate a multi-level nested logit (firm within capability tier) to properly separate the effects, or (c) clearly flag these estimates as uninformative upper bounds rather than drawing policy conclusions from them.

5. **The staggered TWFE estimator promised in the identification memo is not implemented.** The pre-analysis plan and identification memo both list Sun & Abraham (2021) interaction-weighted estimator as a planned robustness check for staggered treatment heterogeneity. This check does not appear in the paper. The paper's defense — that TWFE weighting is less consequential when the aggregate coefficient is zero — is valid for the aggregate same-firm entry coefficient but not for the family-upgrade coefficient of -0.43, which is the result that matters. With 43 upgrade events at different dates and potentially heterogeneous treatment effects, TWFE bias could be substantial. The authors must implement the planned robust estimator for the family-upgrade specification.

6. **The entry variable construction conflates qualitatively different events.** The same-firm entry count in a 7-day window (SD = 8.4) treats a minor model variant identically to a major flagship launch. The aggregate null result may reflect averaging over many inconsequential entries and a few consequential ones. The authors should (a) weight entry events by the entering model's initial uptake; (b) distinguish between "major" entries and minor entries; and (c) report the entry effect using a binary indicator rather than a count, to avoid the implicit linearity assumption.

### Minor Comments（建议回应）

1. **The outside option construction is underspecified.** The paper sets s_0t = 0.20 based on an assumption, then claims sigma is invariant to this choice. But the invariance result (sigma identical to three decimals at 10%, 20%, 40%) is suspicious — it suggests the day FE are absorbing all the variation that the outside option would contribute, which raises questions about whether the outside option is adding any identifying variation or is merely a normalization.

2. **Price endogeneity is not addressed.** The log-price coefficient alpha = 0.26 (nested logit OLS) is presented without discussion of price endogeneity. If higher-quality models charge higher prices and quality is partially unobserved, OLS will attenuate the price coefficient. The paper should at minimum flag this as a limitation.

3. **The rival entry coefficient in Table 3 Column 2 is positive and significant (0.001, SE = 0.0003).** The paper explains this as reflecting market growth coinciding with rival entry, but if day FE properly absorb aggregate demand trends, how can rival entry (which varies only at the date level) show a significant coefficient? This suggests either that the rival entry variable has within-day cross-model variation or that the day FE are not fully absorbing date-level variation.

4. **The Oster delta = 155 is reported without adequate context.** The Oster (2019) test requires specifying R-max. The paper does not state the assumed R-max. Report the R-max and conduct sensitivity with R-max = 1.3×R-tilde and R-max = min(2×R-tilde, 1).

5. **The nested logit uses N = 26,444 vs. 30,903 for the panel.** The difference (~4,459 observations, ~14% of the sample) is due to restricting to observations where the within-firm share is well-defined. Report how many models are lost and whether these excluded models differ systematically.

6. **No discussion of model deprecation in the family-upgrade specification.** The -0.43 coefficient may conflate voluntary switching with forced migration due to provider-side deprecation. How many of the 43 upgrade events involve explicit deprecation of the predecessor?

7. **Day-of-week effects are mentioned but not formally addressed.** Day FE absorb calendar-date effects, not day-of-week effects per se. If entry events are non-randomly distributed across days of the week, the day-of-week cyclicality could interact with entry timing.

8. **The event study figure for family upgrades shows positive coefficients in days 0-7 post-entry.** This suggests predecessor models actually receive MORE traffic initially after an upgrade launches. This is not discussed — it could reflect increased attention, testing/comparison behavior, or measurement artifacts.

### 建议补充的分析

1. **Roth (2022) pre-trend bias correction** for the family-upgrade event study using the HonestDiD package.

2. **Permutation inference for the family-upgrade coefficient.** Randomly reassign the 43 upgrade events to different model-date pairs (within the same firm) and re-estimate the coefficient 1,000+ times.

3. **Sun & Abraham (2021) or Callaway & Sant'Anna (2021) estimator** for the family-upgrade specification to address staggered TWFE bias.

4. **Leave-one-event-out analysis** for the family-upgrade coefficient to identify which individual upgrade events contribute most to the -0.43 estimate.

5. **Hausman-type instruments** for the nested logit: use characteristics of other firms' models as instruments for within-firm share.

6. **Revenue-weighted displacement analysis** using the available pricing data.

7. **Multi-level nested logit (firm within capability tier)** to properly separate within-firm and within-tier substitution margins.

8. **Wild cluster bootstrap** for the family-upgrade coefficient given the small number of effective clusters.

### 总体建议：MAJOR REVISION

The paper addresses an interesting and timely question about competitive dynamics in the LLM API market, and the dual-strategy approach (structural + descriptive) is well-conceived. The writing is clear and the institutional knowledge is strong. However, the significant pre-trends in the family-upgrade event study — which directly contradict the paper's claims and undermine the key finding — must be addressed before publication. The OLS nesting parameter lacks credible identification given the IV failure, and the paper needs additional inference procedures (permutation tests, robust TWFE estimators) to support its main claims. With appropriate corrections and additional analyses, this could become a solid contribution to the empirical IO literature on technology markets.
