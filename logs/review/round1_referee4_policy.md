## Referee Report — Policy & Relevance

**Date**: 2026-03-26
**Paper**: "Creative Destruction in the Market for Intelligence: Demand Reallocation When New LLMs Enter"

### 总体评价（Summary Assessment）

This paper addresses a question of genuine importance to competition policy in AI markets: when a new LLM enters, does it steal demand from rivals, cannibalize the entrant's own models, or expand the market? The answer matters for antitrust enforcement, merger review, and the design of regulatory frameworks for foundation model providers. The paper's central finding -- that creative destruction operates almost exclusively through within-family upgrades, with no detectable cross-firm displacement even under near-zero switching costs -- is striking and, if robust, has significant implications for how we think about market power and competitive dynamics in AI. It suggests that the LLM API market, at least in its current growth phase, is one where firms compete primarily with their own prior generations rather than with each other. For a policymaker considering whether this market is "contestable," this is an important data point.

However, I have substantial concerns about the paper's ability to deliver on its policy promise. The descriptive nature of the evidence -- which the authors commendably acknowledge -- limits the strength of any policy inference. More fundamentally, the paper documents patterns during a 93-day window of explosive market growth (20% expansion in three months), and the authors do not adequately grapple with how dramatically different the competitive dynamics might look once growth slows. The absence of cross-firm displacement during a boom is not the same as durable horizontal differentiation -- it may simply be that nobody needs to steal share when the pie is growing fast enough. The paper's policy discussion (Section 7.4), while appropriately cautious in tone, nonetheless invites readers to draw implications about market power and competitive discipline that the evidence cannot support. The "so what" question -- what should a regulator, platform operator, or developer actually do with these findings -- remains underspecified.

Additionally, the paper's reliance on a single platform (OpenRouter) with a highly non-representative user base (developers who self-select into a multi-model aggregator) severely limits the external validity of any policy conclusion. The paper acknowledges this but does not fully reckon with its consequences: the OpenRouter population is precisely the segment of the market where we would least expect lock-in and most expect rapid switching. Finding no cross-firm displacement here, where switching costs are essentially zero, tells us surprisingly little about whether the same holds for enterprise customers with deep integrations, compliance requirements, and relationship-specific investments.

### 逐项评分

1. **政策相关性：6/10** — The research question is highly policy-relevant. Antitrust authorities in the US, EU, and elsewhere are actively grappling with how to define markets and assess competitive dynamics in foundation model provision. The decomposition into cannibalization, cross-firm displacement, and market expansion directly maps onto the conceptual framework used in merger review and dominance assessments. However, the paper's policy discussion (Section 7.4) is thin -- just three paragraphs offering "observations" rather than a structured engagement with the actual regulatory debates underway. The paper does not discuss market definition (a central antitrust concept), does not engage with the EU AI Act or the FTC's ongoing investigations into foundation model providers, and does not consider how its findings interact with vertical integration concerns (e.g., Google integrating Gemini into Search, or Microsoft integrating OpenAI models into Azure and Office). The claim that "self-competition is the primary discipline on market power" (Section 7.4) is a strong policy statement that is not adequately supported by a 93-day descriptive analysis during a boom period.

2. **外部有效性：4/10** — This is the paper's most significant weakness from a policy perspective. The authors acknowledge that OpenRouter users are not representative (Section 2.2, Section 5.4, Section 7.5), but the implications are not fully developed. OpenRouter represents a specific market segment: API-savvy developers who value multi-model flexibility and who can switch models by changing a single parameter. This is the population with the lowest switching costs in the entire LLM ecosystem. Enterprise customers -- who account for the bulk of LLM revenue -- face switching costs that include custom fine-tuning, compliance audits, rate-limit negotiations, prompt engineering investments, and organizational inertia. The paper's finding that "even in a setting with near-zero switching costs" there is no cross-firm displacement is presented as evidence of horizontal differentiation, but an equally plausible interpretation is that the growth-phase market simply has not yet reached the point where displacement is necessary. Furthermore, OpenRouter's pricing structure (pass-through with a markup) may distort the price variation that drives the nested logit estimates relative to direct API pricing. The paper would benefit from explicit discussion of how each finding would or would not generalize to (a) enterprise customers with direct API contracts, (b) consumer-facing applications where the end user does not choose the model, and (c) markets outside the US where regulatory environments differ.

3. **机制清晰度：5/10** — The paper documents a clear pattern -- within-family upgrades drive displacement, everything else does not -- but the mechanism remains underspecified. Why exactly do users switch to a successor model within the same family but not to a potentially superior model from a rival? The paper offers two candidate explanations (vertical differentiation along quality ladders, and residual switching costs from "evaluating a different model's strengths, adjusting prompt strategies"), but does not adjudicate between them. This matters enormously for policy: if the mechanism is genuine horizontal differentiation (models from different firms serve genuinely different needs), then the absence of cross-firm displacement reflects efficient market segmentation. If the mechanism is residual switching costs or information frictions (developers do not bother evaluating rivals because it is costly to learn a new model's idiosyncrasies), then there may be a role for policy intervention to reduce these frictions and promote competition. The nested logit nesting parameter of 0.46 tells us that within-firm substitution is stronger, but it does not tell us why. The IV failure (negative sigma) is itself informative -- as the authors note, product-line breadth correlates with unobserved firm quality -- but the paper does not pursue this implication. If firms with more models are just better firms (better brand, better ecosystem, better documentation), then "within-firm substitution" partly reflects firm quality rather than switching cost reduction. The paper needs a sharper discussion of competing mechanisms and, ideally, tests that distinguish between them.

4. **福利含义：4/10** — The paper explicitly declines to conduct welfare analysis (Section 7.5: "we cannot assess the welfare implications of the cannibalization pattern") due to the absence of a supply-side model and cost data. This is appropriate given the limitations, but it also means the paper leaves the most policy-relevant question unanswered. Is the observed pattern of rapid within-family upgrade and no cross-firm displacement good or bad for consumers? On one hand, rapid self-improvement benefits users who get better models. On the other hand, if cross-firm displacement is absent because of differentiation rather than superiority, consumers may be "trapped" in suboptimal provider ecosystems even when better alternatives exist. The paper does not discuss consumer surplus implications of the observed price elasticities (which are relatively low at -0.5 to -1.5), does not discuss whether the power-law distribution of usage is efficient or reflects information failures, and does not consider distributional effects across developer types (sophisticated multi-model users vs. single-provider dependents). The observation that "models competing on the same capability-price frontier face intense competition" (Section 7.4) has welfare implications that the paper does not explore: does this intense within-tier competition lead to efficient pricing, or does it lead to a race to the bottom on price with underinvestment in quality?

5. **研究持久价值：5/10** — The paper studies a market in its infancy during a period of exceptional growth. The 93-day window (December 2025 to March 2026) captures a single phase of market evolution. Several features of the current environment may change rapidly: (a) the market is growing at ~20% per quarter, which cannot continue indefinitely; (b) the competitive landscape is in flux, with Chinese providers (DeepSeek, Qwen, Minimax) gaining share rapidly (visible in Figure 4); (c) the technology itself is evolving, with reasoning capabilities, tool use, and multimodal inputs still in early adoption; and (d) regulatory frameworks are actively under development. The paper's main finding -- no cross-firm displacement -- may simply reflect the growth phase, as the authors themselves note. Historical precedent from Bresnahan and Greenstein (1999) suggests that cross-platform displacement in computing took years to materialize. The paper's contribution as a snapshot of early-stage dynamics has value, but the shelf life of its specific estimates is likely short. The methodological contribution (applying nested logit to LLM markets, the cannibalization decomposition framework) is more durable than the empirical findings. The paper would age better if it more explicitly framed its contribution as establishing a methodological template and a baseline measurement that future work can compare against, rather than as a characterization of how this market "works."

### 总分：4.8/10

### Major Comments（必须回应）

1. **The "so what" of no cross-firm displacement needs much sharper articulation.** The null result on cross-firm displacement is the paper's most policy-relevant finding, but the paper does not adequately distinguish between benign and concerning explanations. Is the absence of cross-firm displacement because (a) horizontal differentiation is genuine and efficient (different models genuinely serve different needs), (b) the market is growing so fast that displacement is unnecessary (everyone can grow without stealing share), or (c) information frictions or behavioral inertia prevent developers from switching even when they should? These three explanations have radically different policy implications. Explanation (a) suggests the market is working well. Explanation (b) suggests the finding is transient and will reverse when growth slows -- making the paper's conclusions fragile. Explanation (c) suggests a potential market failure. The paper currently conflates (a) and (b) and does not consider (c). I would like to see: (i) a discussion of what the cross-firm displacement coefficient would look like under each scenario, and (ii) any available evidence that could distinguish between them (e.g., do cross-firm displacement effects appear for models that entered earlier in the sample when growth was slower?).

2. **The pre-trends in the event studies are concerning and inadequately discussed.** The updated_results_summary.csv reports pre-trend t-statistics of -2.46 (same-firm), -2.05 (cross-firm), and -2.75 (family upgrade). These are all statistically significant at conventional levels, meaning the parallel trends assumption is violated. The paper's text (Section 6.3) states that "pre-event coefficients are noisy but centered near zero" for the same-firm event study, but the formal pre-trend test tells a different story. Figure 15 visually shows pre-trend oscillation, and Figure 16 (family upgrades) shows large negative pre-event coefficients (around -1 to -1.5) well before the upgrade date. If predecessor model usage was already declining before the successor's release, the family-upgrade coefficient of -0.43 may be capturing a pre-existing trend rather than a causal effect of entry. The paper must: (i) report and discuss the pre-trend test statistics prominently, not bury them in auxiliary files; (ii) consider whether the pre-existing decline in predecessor usage actually *triggers* the decision to release a successor (reverse causality); and (iii) assess whether the -0.43 coefficient is attenuated or amplified once pre-trends are accounted for.

3. **The IV failure deserves more attention as a substantive finding, not just a methodological inconvenience.** The negative IV estimate of sigma (-0.15) is outside the parameter space, and the paper correctly attributes this to violation of the exclusion restriction. But the paper too quickly moves on. The IV failure tells us something important: in this market, the standard BLP instrument logic does not work because product-line breadth is endogenous to firm quality. This is not just a technical problem -- it undermines the entire identifying variation for the nested logit. If firms with more models are systematically higher-quality (better APIs, better documentation, stronger brand), then the OLS sigma of 0.46 may be substantially biased. The Oster bound of 155 addresses general omitted variable bias but does not address the specific simultaneity concern that the authors themselves identify. The paper should: (i) provide bounds on sigma under plausible assumptions about the direction and magnitude of the simultaneity bias; (ii) explore alternative instruments (e.g., regulatory shocks, exogenous model deprecations, changes in compute costs); and (iii) consider whether a moment inequality approach might be informative even when point identification fails.

4. **Market definition is conspicuously absent from a paper with policy aspirations.** The paper uses OpenRouter as the "market" but never formally discusses market definition -- a prerequisite for any antitrust analysis. Are all 385 models in one market? The near-unity nesting parameters under capability-tier grouping suggest that reasoning and non-reasoning models are in separate markets. Are open-source and closed-source models in the same market? Are models at different price tiers in the same market? The paper's own evidence (Table 5) points toward multiple relevant markets segmented by capability and price, but this implication is not drawn out. For a paper claiming policy relevance, an explicit discussion of market definition using the SSNIP test framework or the hypothetical monopolist test would substantially strengthen the contribution.

5. **The family-upgrade classification is endogenous and potentially circular.** The paper's key finding depends entirely on distinguishing "same-family upgrades" from "different-family entries," but this classification is based on string matching of model names. This is problematic for two reasons. First, naming conventions are strategic -- firms choose names to signal continuity or discontinuity with prior models, and this choice may correlate with expected demand reallocation. A firm that expects its new model to cannibalize an old one may name it as an upgrade (GPT-4o-mini); a firm that wants to expand into a new segment may give it a new name. The classification thus partly reflects firm expectations about displacement rather than independently classifying the competitive relationship. Second, with only 43 upgrade events (and only 8 with sufficient pre/post data for the event study), the results may be driven by a handful of high-profile releases from a few firms. The paper should: (i) provide sensitivity to alternative classification schemes (e.g., based on capability overlap rather than name matching); (ii) report which specific upgrade events drive the -0.43 coefficient; and (iii) discuss whether the classification could be endogenous.

### Minor Comments（建议回应）

1. **The HHI trajectory in Figure 2 is misleading and warrants explanation.** The HHI drops from nearly 10,000 to below 1,500 in the first two weeks of the sample. This dramatic decline almost certainly reflects the panel's construction (starting with a small number of observed models and ramping up as the scraper captures more models), not a genuine decline in concentration. If the initial HHI values are an artifact of data availability rather than market structure, they should be dropped or clearly labeled. Reporting time-averaged HHI only for the period after the panel stabilizes (say, January 2026 onward) would be more informative.

2. **The paper should discuss multi-homing explicitly.** Fradkin (2025) emphasizes widespread multi-homing on OpenRouter. If developers routinely use multiple models simultaneously (rather than choosing one), the nested logit's discrete-choice framework is misspecified. The paper should discuss (a) the prevalence of multi-homing in this setting, (b) how multi-homing affects the interpretation of the nesting parameter, and (c) whether a model of demand with multihoming (e.g., continuous demand allocation rather than discrete choice) would be more appropriate.

3. **Revenue-weighted analysis is needed.** The paper uses request counts as the primary usage measure, but from a welfare and policy perspective, revenue (or token volume times price) is more relevant. A model receiving many cheap requests may matter less for competition than a model receiving fewer expensive requests. The robustness check using log tokens (Table 6, row 1) addresses this partially, but a full analysis using revenue-weighted shares would be more informative for policy.

4. **The role of free/subsidized models should be discussed.** Some models on OpenRouter may be offered at below-cost prices (subsidized by providers seeking adoption). If DeepSeek or other Chinese providers are subsidizing usage to gain market share, the observed demand patterns reflect distorted prices. This is directly policy-relevant (it is the subject of ongoing trade policy debates about Chinese AI subsidies) and the paper should at least acknowledge the issue.

5. **The paper does not discuss data moats or ecosystem lock-in.** Even if switching models on OpenRouter is technically costless, developers accumulate model-specific knowledge (prompting strategies, output parsing, performance expectations) that creates soft lock-in. The paper's framework treats this as part of the "horizontal differentiation" captured by sigma, but it is qualitatively different -- it is an endogenous switching cost that grows with usage. This distinction matters for policy: if lock-in is accumulating even on a low-switching-cost platform, it suggests that the market may become less contestable over time even without formal barriers.

6. **Table 2, Column 3 (NL-IV) should report the Anderson-Rubin confidence set for sigma**, not just the point estimate, since the parameter is near the boundary of the theoretically admissible space and the IV may be weak in the relevant sense despite the large first-stage F.

7. **The weekday effect (Figure 10, Section 4.4) suggests that the "consumers" in this model are automated pipelines, not human developers making daily choices.** This has implications for the nested logit interpretation: if demand is driven by scheduled batch processing rather than active model selection, the substitution patterns reflect engineering choices made at setup time, not daily switching behavior. The paper should discuss this.

### 政策建议补充

1. **Vertical integration and self-preferencing.** Several model providers (Google, Microsoft/OpenAI, Amazon/Anthropic) are also cloud infrastructure providers and application platforms. The observation that competition is primarily intra-firm has a different policy implication when the "firm" is also the cloud platform, the search engine, and the productivity suite. Vertical foreclosure concerns -- e.g., Google preferencing Gemini models in Search or Cloud -- are not addressed but are directly relevant to the paper's finding that firm boundaries define the primary competitive margin.

2. **Interoperability and portability regulation.** The EU's Digital Markets Act and proposed AI Act include provisions on interoperability. The paper's finding that near-zero switching costs on OpenRouter do not produce cross-firm displacement is directly relevant to debates about whether mandating interoperability would promote competition. If switching costs are already near zero and displacement still does not occur, then interoperability mandates may be insufficient -- the barrier to competition is differentiation, not lock-in. Alternatively, if the OpenRouter population is unrepresentative, the finding says little about whether interoperability would help the broader market.

3. **Innovation incentives under cannibalization dominance.** The paper observes that firms "must keep upgrading to retain their user base" (Section 7.4) but does not discuss Arrow (1962) vs. Schumpeter in depth. If firms primarily compete with themselves, the replacement effect predicts underinvestment in innovation relative to the social optimum. But if the observed rapid upgrade cadence is itself driven by the threat of eventual cross-firm displacement (even if not yet observed), then innovation incentives may be approximately efficient. The paper's evidence cannot distinguish these scenarios, but the discussion should lay out the theoretical possibilities.

4. **Concentration in upstream inputs.** The paper focuses on the downstream API market, but concentration in upstream inputs (GPU supply from NVIDIA, training data, AI talent) may be more consequential for long-run competition. The paper's finding of moderate downstream concentration (HHI ~1,524) coexists with extreme upstream concentration that the paper does not discuss. A policy-oriented paper should at least acknowledge that downstream competitive dynamics are constrained by upstream bottlenecks.

5. **Open-source dynamics and their regulatory implications.** Figure 4 shows DeepSeek and other open-source providers gaining share rapidly. The competitive dynamics between open-source and closed-source models -- including the possibility that open-source models discipline closed-source pricing -- is a first-order policy question that the paper's framework could potentially address (e.g., by estimating separate nesting parameters for open-source vs. closed-source models) but does not.

### 总体建议：MAJOR REVISION

The paper addresses an important and timely question with a reasonable empirical approach, and the core finding -- that displacement is concentrated in within-family upgrades -- is interesting and novel as a systematic result. However, the paper's policy relevance is undermined by (1) inadequate engagement with the specific policy debates where these findings would matter, (2) serious external validity limitations that are acknowledged but not fully reckoned with, (3) troubling pre-trend violations that are not transparently reported, (4) the absence of welfare analysis or market definition analysis, and (5) insufficient discussion of the mechanisms that would allow a policymaker to act on the findings. A major revision that sharpens the mechanism discussion, honestly addresses the pre-trend concerns, engages seriously with market definition and the specific regulatory frameworks under development, and provides explicit bounds on the generalizability of findings beyond OpenRouter would substantially improve the paper's contribution to the policy-relevant literature on AI market competition.
